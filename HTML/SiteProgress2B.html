This page is to showcase a few snippets of my brainstorming, ideation, prototyping and coding processes. Though not every step is shown, the most important or pertinent have been highlighted to encapsulate a general idea of my process and major turning points for the project.

Drawing with the mouse

I practised working with gradually fading lines, however I didn't like that to get rid of the drawing we just drew a new background, so I looked into the 'clear' function instead.

Welcome Message

I immediately knew I wanted to integrate my P5 learnings into my website by creating a 'landing page', that wasnt just the main navigation, or contents, page.

Text to Points Learning

A large chunk of my P5 learning came from making my Welcome Message in P5. It was more of a challenge than I initally expected and it stretched my coding abilities to their limit. I learnt an immense amount and feel really confident after implementing some of the techniques I learnt in this video.

Problem: Messy Letters

One of the issues I ran into when making this animation was the 'furry' appearance of the text, that occured due to way the algorithm would work out what the 'next point' in the array to throw a 'dart' at is. This was fixed after troubleshooting by increasing a 'closeness' tolerance value.

How the Welcome Message Works

The way the welcome message works, is by creating a target array of points, made from the text to points function, and then a second 'dart' array, to move points toward their matching targets. I spent alot of time on this code, and learning about linear interpolation, or lerp, was really intersting to me.

Problem: Joined Letters

Another problem I encountered when making the welcome message was the ability for the code to tell when one letter ends and another starts. This led to joined letters that did not look good. The solution to this issue was in telling the code how close the 'distBetween' the two points, previous and current dart, could be determined as most probable to be a different letter or stroke.

Drawing on Canvas

Previously in 2A I had tried to code teh ability to draw on the screen with a mouse by using vanilla JS and HTML to create a canvas. The code was complicated and the functionality was inconsistent. I also found it frustrating that I couldn't draw over or under other HTML elements. When I learnt P5, I knew I was going to update this, so now I implemented the drawing functionality for users when they first arrive, and they can draw in the sketchbook next to the welcome message.

Random Scribbles

The next thing I looked into with P5 was the possibity of drawing random lines. I had a random colour being selected for the link on hover, and I like the idea of having more things that are different each time you visit the website. Completey randomly generated lines like sketchbook scribbling were what I had in mind.

Problem: Rigid Lines

The first issue I encountered while trying to create random scribbles was that they didn't look organic, and were very rigid, with sharp points. This did not look like a human had drawn it in their sketchbook. The solution to this, was learning about curve vertexes. Though there a few quirks, such as anchor points and fill that tripped me up in my learnings, they are a valuable thing to have in my toolkit now.

Problem: Immediately Appearing

My other issue with my inital understanding of P5 was that the lines I was drawing always appeared the entire line at once, rather than appearing to be 'drawn' and going start to finish like a penstroke. The solution here was to change the incrementation of each line segment, keep track of whether the line is finished or not, and ultimately decrease the framerate to slowen the page of the line.

Many Scribbles

I implemented the random scribble as a background on the contents page, and initally let all the scribbles pile ontop of one another. I found this to be too busy.

Set Timeout

I learnt about the Set Timeout functionality, and implemented with a clear function to let the scribbles be generated one at a time.
